# Query Playbook

Response templates for the four standard investor query types.
Load this file when detailed guidance on structuring a response is needed.

---

## Query Type 1: "Is [Stock X] worth investing in now?"

Run all 7 steps in order. Never skip steps; the value of the framework is in
the interaction between dimensions, not any single one.

**Step 1 — Second-level check**
- What is the consensus view on this company?
- How does the current price reflect that consensus?
- What non-consensus insight, if correct, would create alpha?
- *If the investor's view matches consensus exactly, they have no edge.*

**Step 2 — Intrinsic value estimate**
- Frame the range of intrinsic value: P/FCF, EV/EBITDA, simplified DCF
- What growth rate does the current price embed? Is that realistic?
- Identify the 1–2 assumptions that matter most to the valuation

**Step 3 — Price vs. value**
- Is the stock cheap, fair, or expensive relative to intrinsic value?
- What is the margin of safety?
- Compare to: own historical range, sector peers, broader market

**Step 4 — Risk assessment**
- Business risk: can the model break?
- Valuation risk: is perfection priced in?
- Leverage risk: company debt + investor leverage
- Macro/cycle risk: late-cycle exposure?
- Psychological risk: is consensus enthusiasm creating hidden fragility?
- Identify the scenario that causes *permanent* loss

**Step 5 — Cycle and sentiment position**
- Where is the pendulum for this stock / sector?
- Fearful, neutral, or euphoric?
- Stage of the relevant cycle (credit, industry, business)?

**Step 6 — Contrarian test**
Check the bargain checklist (see dimensions.md → Dimension 8):
- Universally loved → be cautious; upside is priced in, downside is not
- Widely hated but fundamentally sound → look closer

**Step 7 — Verdict**
Always conditional, never binary:

> "At current price [$X], given assumptions [A, B, C], the risk-reward appears
> [attractive / fair / unattractive] because [reason]. This assessment would change if
> [condition 1] or [condition 2]."

Never say "buy" or "don't buy" without explicit conditions and assumptions.

## Query Type 2: "What should I research before investing in [Company X]?"

Structure the response around six areas. For each area, give the investor
specific questions to answer — not just categories.

**1. Business model and moat**
- How exactly does the company make money? Is revenue recurring or transactional?
- What prevents a competitor from copying this? (patents, switching costs, network effects, cost advantages, brand)
- Is the moat widening or narrowing?

**2. Free cash flow and balance sheet**
- What is normalized free cash flow? (strip out one-time items)
- What is the debt load? Can it be serviced if earnings fall 30%?
- Any covenant risks or refinancing walls in the next 2–3 years?
- ROIC vs. cost of capital — is the business creating or destroying value?

**3. Management and capital allocation**
- What is the track record on acquisitions? Were they value-accretive?
- Does management buy back stock at good prices, or just to offset dilution?
- How aligned is insider ownership with shareholder interests?
- Do they acknowledge problems honestly in earnings calls and letters?

**4. Valuation**
- Absolute: P/E, P/FCF, EV/EBITDA vs. own 5-year history
- Relative: vs. closest peers
- Implied: what revenue/earnings growth does the current price embed?
- Scenario analysis: what is the stock worth in bull / base / bear case?

**5. Sentiment and ownership**
- What is analyst consensus? Are all 30 analysts "Buy"? (uniformity = fragility)
- Is institutional ownership rising or falling?
- Short interest: why are shorts there? Is their thesis credible?

**6. Bear case — steelman it**
- What would have to be true for this to permanently impair capital?
- What are the 2–3 risks that get the least attention in the bull thesis?
- Has the investor considered a scenario where their key assumption is wrong?

## Query Type 3: "When should I buy / sell [Stock X]?"

**Buying decision framework**

Green lights (all should be present for a strong buy):
- Price is meaningfully below your estimate of intrinsic value
- Sentiment is fearful, pessimistic, or simply indifferent
- Business fundamentals are intact despite the negative narrative
- You understand the catalyst or path to value recognition (even if timing is uncertain)
- The position feels uncomfortable — it is contrarian and unpopular

Yellow lights (partial, proceed with smaller position):
- Price is modestly below value but margin of safety is thin
- Sentiment is neutral, not fearful
- Thesis is sound but not differentiated from consensus

Red lights (do not buy):
- Price fully reflects the optimistic scenario
- Everyone likes it and expects further appreciation
- You are buying because the price has gone up
- The thesis requires multiple things to go right simultaneously

**Selling decision framework**

Sell when:
- Price has reached or exceeded your intrinsic value estimate
- The original thesis is disproved by new facts (not just price action)
- A clearly superior opportunity exists (relative selection)
- Sentiment has shifted from fear/hate to love/euphoria
- Risk has increased materially (new debt, regulatory threat, competitive disruption)

Do NOT sell because:
- Price has declined (unless the underlying thesis has changed)
- Others are selling and you feel uncomfortable
- The investment "hasn't worked" yet — patience is required

*Key principle:* Sell decisions should be driven by re-evaluating the thesis and
comparing to alternatives, not by short-term price movements or emotions.

## Query Type 4: "Is now a good time to invest in general?"

Run the full market thermometer. Synthesize into a positioning recommendation.

**Credit market signals:**
- Investment-grade and high-yield spreads vs. long-run averages
- Covenant quality in new debt issuances
- Pace of leveraged buyout activity
- Bank lending standards (tightening or loosening?)

**Equity market signals:**
- Broad market P/E vs. 20-year median
- IPO volume and quality (are companies coming public profitable or speculative?)
- M&A multiples — are buyers paying rational prices?
- Earnings revision trend (upgrades vs. downgrades)

**Sentiment signals:**
- Investor surveys: bull/bear ratios
- Cash allocation: are managers fully invested or holding dry powder?
- Media coverage tone: fear or euphoria?
- Retail investor participation: rising sharply = late-cycle signal

**Synthesis — positioning output:**

| Reading | Recommendation |
|---------|----------------|
| All signals: cheap, fearful, wide spreads | Deploy capital aggressively; this is the rare time to be bold |
| Mixed signals, mostly fair | Selective; require high conviction; keep some dry powder |
| Mostly expensive, complacent | Reduce risk; emphasize quality; hold cash; resist FOMO |
| All signals: expensive, euphoric, tight spreads | Defensive; capital preservation > return; the cycle will turn |

Always note: even in an expensive market, specific sectors or individual securities
may be cheap. Market-level assessment sets the posture; security selection still matters.
