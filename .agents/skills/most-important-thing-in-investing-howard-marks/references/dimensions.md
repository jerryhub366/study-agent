# Dimensions Reference

Full detail for all 11 Howard Marks investment dimensions.
Load this file when deeper guidance on any dimension is needed.

---

## Dimension 1: Second-Level Thinking

**Rule:** To outperform, your view must be *different from consensus AND more correct.*

First-level: "NVIDIA is a great company — buy it."
Second-level: "NVIDIA is great, but everyone knows it. The price reflects perfection.
Any disappointment will be punished hard. Is the risk-reward still attractive?"

**Questions to ask:**
1. What does consensus think about this company?
2. How does my assessment differ from consensus?
3. What is the probability my non-consensus view is correct?
4. If consensus is right, what is the price already reflecting?
5. If I'm right, how much can I gain? If I'm wrong, how much do I lose?

Outstanding returns come only from *correct non-consensus views.*
For popular or obvious investments, always surface: *What does the current price already assume?*

## Dimension 2: Intrinsic Value

**Rule:** Without an accurate estimate of intrinsic value, all else is speculation.

Value derives from (in order of reliability):
1. **Free cash flow generation** — most important; how much, how reliably?
2. **Hard assets** — book value, tangible assets, net asset value
3. **Earnings power** — current and normalized earnings
4. **Growth potential** — uncertain; must be priced to reflect that uncertainty
5. **Competitive moat** — patents, brands, switching costs, network effects
6. **Management quality** — capital allocation track record

Value vs. Growth: the real choice is *value today vs. value tomorrow.*
Growth must be bought at a price that reflects its uncertainty.

**The Nifty Fifty warning:** IBM, Xerox, Kodak — great companies, destroyed investors
who paid 80–90x earnings. Price paid always matters, regardless of quality.

Always begin: estimate what the business is worth, then compare to price.

## Dimension 3: Price vs. Value

**Rule:** Investment success = buying things well, not buying good things.
There is no such thing as a good or bad investment regardless of price.

- "Well bought is half sold." Buy at a sufficient discount; the exit takes care of itself.
- The worst asset can be a great investment if cheap enough.
- The best asset is a terrible investment if too expensive.

**Price determinants beyond fundamentals:**
1. Psychology — dominant short-term driver
2. Technicals — forced selling (margin calls), forced buying (fund inflows)
3. Fundamentals — dominant long-term driver

**Popularity test:** The most dangerous thing is buying at peak popularity.
When all favorable facts are priced in, no new buyers remain.

**Key valuation questions:**
- P/E, P/FCF, EV/EBITDA vs. historical range and peers?
- What growth rate does the current price imply? Is that realistic?
- Is the asset more loved or less loved than 12–24 months ago?
- Are people buying on merit, or because it has been going up?

## Dimension 4: Risk

**Rule:** Risk = probability of permanent loss of capital, NOT volatility.

- Higher volatility ≠ higher risk if the business is fundamentally sound.
- An "apparently safe" asset priced too high IS risky.
- The most dangerous investments feel safe because of recent performance.

**Seven forms of risk:**
1. **Fundamental business risk** — Can the business model collapse?
2. **Valuation risk** — Is perfection priced in?
3. **Leverage risk** — Can debt force selling at the worst moment?
4. **Liquidity risk** — Can you exit when needed?
5. **Macro/cycle risk** — Exposed to a late-cycle credit boom?
6. **Psychological risk** — Is the investor swept up in crowd enthusiasm?
7. **Career/benchmark risk** — (for professionals) underperformance vs. peers

**The risk paradox:**
- When everyone thinks it's risky → prices fall → it becomes *less* risky.
- When everyone thinks it's safe → prices rise → it becomes *more* risky.

**High-risk environment signals:** universal optimism, cheap credit, weak covenants,
"this time it's different" language, compressed risk premiums, leverage everywhere.

**Lower-risk environment signals:** widespread fear, tight credit, cheap assets,
wide risk premiums, investors selling regardless of price.

## Dimension 5: Market Cycles

**Rule:** Everything is cyclical. No trend goes on forever.
"Trees don't grow to the sky. Few things go to zero."

**The credit cycle (Marks's favorite):**
```
Economy improves
→ Lenders loosen standards, capital flows freely
→ Speculation and leverage build
→ Loss events occur
→ Lenders panic, credit dries up
→ Defaults, fire sales → opportunity for disciplined buyers
→ Economy stabilizes → repeat
```

**Bull market 3 stages:**
1. A few thoughtful investors see recovery is possible
2. Most investors recognize improvement and buy in
3. Everyone concludes things will only get better ← danger zone

**Bear market 3 stages:**
1. A few investors see things won't stay rosy
2. Most recognize deterioration
3. Everyone convinced things can only get worse ← opportunity zone

Never extrapolate trends. When people say "the cycle is over," bet against them.

## Dimension 6: The Pendulum

**Rule:** Investor psychology oscillates between extremes. The midpoint is where
it *should* be, but it spends most time at the extremes.

Swings between: euphoria ↔ depression, greed ↔ fear, risk-seeking ↔ risk-aversion,
overpriced ↔ underpriced.

- The swing back from an extreme is usually faster than the swing to it.
- We never know how far the pendulum will swing, or when it will reverse.
- Stocks are cheapest when everything looks grim; most expensive when everything looks great.

Action: assess where the pendulum is today for each asset and for the market overall.

## Dimension 7: Psychological Traps

**Rule:** The biggest errors come not from bad analysis, but from psychological failure.

| Trap | Signal |
|------|--------|
| **Greed** | Reaching for return without acknowledging risk |
| **FOMO** | "I have to own this before it goes higher" |
| **Herding** | Doing what everyone else does; comfort in consensus |
| **Recency bias** | Extrapolating recent trends: "it's gone up so it must be good" |
| **Confirmation bias** | Only absorbing information that supports existing view |
| **Silver bullet** | Believing a new strategy offers high return with low risk |
| **Capitulation** | Selling at lows because the pain is unbearable |

When an investor asks about a hot stock or popular trend, name which trap(s)
may be affecting their judgment.

## Dimension 8: Contrarianism & Finding Bargains

**Rule:** The best opportunities are found among things most others won't do.
If buying is comfortable and consensus, the upside is already priced in.

**Bargain checklist — a true bargain typically shows all of:**
- [ ] Ignored or scorned by media and most investors
- [ ] Price has been falling; "who would want to own that?"
- [ ] Unappreciated, unpopular, unloved
- [ ] Trailing record of poor returns
- [ ] Recently subject to disinvestment, not accumulation
- [ ] **Perception is considerably worse than reality**

**Contrarian trap:** Don't be contrarian just to be different. You must understand
*why* the crowd is wrong. Contrarianism based on analysis is profitable;
based on stubbornness it is dangerous.

**Full due diligence checklist:**

*Business fundamentals:*
- Revenue model and recurring revenue quality
- Free cash flow generation (current and normalized)
- Balance sheet: debt levels, cash, covenant risk
- Return on invested capital (ROIC)

*Competitive position:*
- Moat: patents, brands, switching costs, network effects, cost advantages
- Market share trend: gaining or losing ground?
- Threat of disruption: technology, regulation, substitutes

*Management:*
- Capital allocation history: buybacks, acquisitions, dividends at rational prices?
- Insider ownership and incentive alignment
- Candor: do they acknowledge problems honestly?

*Valuation:*
- Absolute: P/E, P/FCF, EV/EBITDA vs. own history
- Relative: vs. industry peers
- Implied growth: what growth rate does the current price embed?
- Scenario analysis: bull / base / bear case price

*Sentiment:*
- Analyst consensus and uniformity (uniformity = risk signal)
- Institutional ownership trend: buying or selling?
- Short interest: large short position for good reason or squeezable?

## Dimension 9: Patient Opportunism

**Rule:** There is not always something great to do. The best strategy is often to wait.

**Buy when:**
- Price meaningfully below intrinsic value
- Sentiment is fearful or indifferent
- Business fundamentals are sound despite negative narrative
- Risk-reward is asymmetric: limited downside, substantial upside
- The position feels uncomfortable and contrarian

**Do NOT buy when:**
- Price fully reflects the optimistic scenario
- Everyone loves it and expects more upside
- You are buying because it has gone up
- The investment requires everything to go right

**Sell when:**
- Price has reached or exceeded intrinsic value
- Original thesis is disproved by new facts
- A clearly better opportunity exists
- Sentiment has shifted from hate to love
- Risk has increased materially

**Do NOT sell merely because:**
- Price has fallen (unless the thesis is broken)
- It feels uncomfortable to hold

*Keynes warning:* "The market can remain irrational longer than you can remain solvent."
A correct view is not enough — you must be able to hold through the wait.

## Dimension 10: Market Environment Assessment

**Rule:** Assess where we are before deciding how aggressively to invest.

**Market thermometer — check all signals:**

Macro:
- Credit availability: tight (defensive signal) or loose (caution signal)?
- Risk premiums: wide (opportunity) or compressed (danger)?
- Leverage in the system: high (danger) or low (opportunity)?
- Economic cycle position: early, mid, or late expansion?

Valuation:
- Broad market P/E vs. historical median
- High-yield spreads vs. long-run average
- Volume of speculative activity (IPOs, M&A, leveraged buyouts)

Sentiment:
- Greed or fear?
- Cash on sidelines or fully deployed?
- Are amateur investors entering the market?

**Positioning table:**

| Environment | Positioning |
|-------------|-------------|
| Cheap + fearful + wide spreads | Aggressive — deploy capital fully |
| Fair + neutral | Selective — high bar, wait for fat pitches |
| Expensive + euphoric + tight spreads | Defensive — raise quality, hold cash |
| Bubble signals | Very defensive — capital preservation priority |

## Dimension 11: Defensive Investing

**Rule:** First goal: do not lose. Asymmetry (more upside than downside capture)
is the hallmark of skilled investing.

Two types of error:
1. **Commission** — buying something that loses money (more common)
2. **Omission** — not buying something that goes up (painful but survivable)

Permanent capital loss is not survivable in the same way as a missed gain.

**Defensive principles:**
- Insist on margin of safety — buy cheap enough to survive being wrong
- Diversify — no single position ruins the portfolio
- Avoid leverage — it magnifies both gains and losses, and can force selling at the worst time
- Stay within circle of competence
- Reject "high return, low risk" arguments — if it sounds too good, something is hidden

**Error formula:**
```
Analytical error in valuation
+ Underestimating range of outcomes
+ Greed, fear, ego, or conformity taken to extreme
→ Price diverges from value
→ Investor fails to notice → loss
```

For every investment thesis: ask explicitly — what is the bear case?
What must be true for this to cause permanent loss?
Is the margin of safety sufficient to survive that scenario?
